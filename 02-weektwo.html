The year is 2089.

.

I live in a home for old people on Mars. I wake up in the morning feeling rhapsodic and youthful. I dress up, brush my hair, and apply my face cream. You feed Buck (or Pluto?), my tri-color Bassett Hound, three strawberries and a teaspoon of milk. My laundry room then explodes. I must evacuate.



● The temperature outside is -243°F (-153°C)
● There is a dust storm with 120 mph winds carrying rocks and spatial debris.
● It's pitch dark except for the light from Phobos and Deimos, Mars's two moons.
● You're out of protein pellets and you need one every 45 minutes.




Well. That's okay. Luckily, as a Minecraft expert, I quickly retrieve my homemade DIY ender portal and push Pluto (or is it Buck?) in. I jump in right after. Now, all I have to do to get to my safe home is defeat the ender dragon and beat the game with no supplies on me. Easy.



But wait! You might be asking... how does one even craft an ender portal? Perhaps you've found yourself in a similar situation. Perhaps you're just curious. Good thing this page gives you the straight DIY directions to do so. As we all know, in the game, ender portals can't be built. They must be found, and then attached to nine eyes of ender. Luckily, in Mars, things work a bit differently. All you really need here is some wooden blocks. As the house crumbles around me, I quickly open Adobe Creative Cloud.

image insert here

After designing the dimensions, creating nine blocks with spaces for the eyes—I set to laser cut out. Once laser cut, I use wood glue to attach the bottom and sides. In order to give the illusion the portal would work, I decided to make an automaton-esque invention:

I would spin gears that, when spun fast enough, would make the portal look alive. Figuring out how to do this was the most difficult part of the entire process. If I could redo the entire process, I would've created the portal and subsequent gears in Adobe and made sure the distances were logical. Because I had decided this on the spur of the moment, I had to measure with a ruler, guess, and hope for the best. Another thing I did not realize was that the prongs of each gear needed to be the same size. That was a hard lesson to learn.

image insert here

In order to create some texture and layers to the portal, I stacked gears on top of each other—creating two levels. When the mother gear was spun, the bottom wheel would spin the parts on the bottom, while the middle/top would spin gears that had been suspended up with a mixture of dowels and tears. Once that was done, I used the wood cutter to cut out several small squares, and glued them together in order to create height. These were my eyes. To make sure the eyes wouldn't just fall down, I glued a wooden dowel underneath all the rules.

Finally, I painted each part, including the gears.
